As part of our calculus project, we wanted to determine the rates of the change in volume, surface area, and radius when a spherical lollipop is submerged into water. When this is done, the sugars in the lollipop dissociate from the rest of the candy's body over time. To do this, we placed a lollipop in water and measured its radius every one (1) minute, rounding all results to the nearest half (0.5) millimeter (mm). This was calculated over the course of twenty (20) minutes.

When dealing with changing aspects of a sphere, we had to refer to the basic equations for calculating the volume and surface area of a sphere, as shown below:

$$V = {4 \over 3} \pi r^3$$

$$SA = 4 \pi r^2$$

These equations can be used to find surface area and volume using radius, but they only apply when the sphere is stationary. In our case , all measurements of our lollipop are constantly changing as the candy dissolves in the water. In order the account for this, equations factoring in the rates of change of the aspects are important:

$${dV \over dt} = {{4 \pi r^2} {dr \over dt}}$$

$${dSA \over dt} = {{8 \pi r} {dr \over dt}}$$

Now that we have equations that solve for the rate of change for our volume and surface area, all that is needed to solve for either is the instantaneous radius and the rate of change of the radius.

In order to find these, we needed to collect data first. In our experiment, we started with a lollipop with a radius of seventeen (17) mm. As we dissolved the candy, we recorded the instantaneous radius at each milestone of one (1) minute. After all data has been collected, we plugged our values into a graph where radius relates to time. Next, we plugged in a best fit line in order to find the average rate of change of the radius, as seen in the expected tab of our data.

Now that the rate of change of the radius has been assigned an understood constant, we can calculate the instantaneous rates of change of the volume and surface area by plugging in our new constant rate for radius and then using the instantaneous radius at any given moment:

$${dV \over dt} = {{4 \pi r^2} {-3 \over 4}}$$

$${dSA \over dt} = {{8 \pi r} {-3 \over 4}}$$

Now, with the data we have collected and the rate of changes almost fully solved for, all that has to be done is for us replace r with the radius given to us at any given moment. These equations can be used as a means for predicting the values of volume, surface area, and the rates of change of volume and surface area.
